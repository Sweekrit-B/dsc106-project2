Proposition: There is a positive association between foreign aid intensity and health burden indicators.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • We grouped our data by country and calculated the mean value for each health burden indicator, using Net ODA received (% of gross capital formation) as our measure of foreign aid. Deception scale: -1.0. We selected this metric because it showed the clearest positive relationship compared to other aid measures. While this helped make the trend more visible it is somewhat deceptive because a different metric may not have produced such a strong or consistent pattern, meaning we are selectively presenting the version that best supports our argument.
  • We grouped our data into hexiles over something like quantiles or quintiles because it showed the most clear positive relationship between all burden indicator and foreign aid received. By grouping our data into ordinal categories we were able to reduce noise and clearly present the positive trends. Deception scale: -0.5. This choice does hide some of the of the variability within each group and can make the relationship appear stronger than it actually. While not inherently deceptive, this choice was made because it produced a clearer and more persuasive pattern.
  • By creating seperate graphs for each disease metric we reduce visual clutter and allow for each indicator to be clearly interpreted. Deception scale: +1.0. Doing this shows a consistent positive relationship across all health burden indicators which helps strengthen our arguement that there is a positive trend between foreign aid received and health burden indicators. This decision was made to improve clarity and does not distort the data, though it may slightly strengthen the viewers perception the positive relationship.
  • The annotations on our chart add an extra persuasive element to our visuals, both annotations help guide the viewers interpretation of the data as well as highlight patterns that support our arguement. Deception scale: -0.5. While the contents of the annotations are true we use them in a way that try to push the narrative more to one side therefore it is slightly deceptive.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • We chose a scatterplot instead of a bar chart to display the continuous relationship between foreign aid and health outcomes. By doing this we can show both the negative relationship and patterns/variability of our data that would have otherwise been hidden with a bar chart. Deception scale: -1.0. When selecting which graph to use, we chose the scatterplot because it revealed a clearer downward trend compared to the bar chart version, which showed little to no relationship. This introduces some bias as we are choosing the visualization that best supports our argument.
  • We applied a log scaling to our data because both the health indicator and foreign aid values were highly skewed. Deception scale: +1.5. Doing this helped compress extreme values and reduce the influence of outliers in our data, making the relationship easier to see. This is a standard and appropriate transformation, though it can make patterns appear smoother and more linear than they are in the raw data.
  • We used a LOWESS regression becuase the relationship between foreign aid and health burden is nonlinear. Deception scale: -1.0. The trend line and R correlation value help guide the viewers interpretation of the data by making the downwards relationship more clear, but it is also used to make the relationship appear stronger than it actually is.
  • As with our first visual the inclusion of the annotations is meant to add an extra persuasive element to the graph and guide the viewer to our argument that, as more foreign aid is recieved, health burden indicators tend to decrease. Deception scale: -1.0. Both annotations highlight specific moments and values to support that increased foreign aid is associated with lower health burden indicators. By directing the viewers attention to certain trends we can influence their interpretation of the graph.

Final Reflection

Throughout our process of making these graphs, we experimented with different types of visualizations as well as data transformations in order to produce different narratives from the same dataset. For our first visual we chose to use a bar chart. By binning our data it made trends much clearer to see and emphasized the message that there is a positive relationship between foreign aid across all health burden indicators. For our second visual by aggregating our data on year and using a scatter plot, we were able to clearly show a downwards relationship between foreign aid and disease burdens which we otherwise would not have seen if we used a bar chart. By being intentional in the choices of how we grouped the data, selected axes, and which foreign aid metric to display, such as using Net ODA received (% of gross capital formation) in the first chart and grants, excluding technical cooperation (BoP, current US$) in the second, we were able to change the message we wanted to communicate.

Specifically, a subtle form of cherry-picking can be observed in our axis choices. Our positive visualization uses ODA as a percentage of gross capital formation on the x axis, a relative measure that normalizes aid by domestic investment size, while we show the absolute health burden indicators on the y axis without equivalent normalization. This irregular formatting could potential suggest that we are hiding information about relative help received. Similarly, the negative visualization uses total grants in log-transformed USD on the x-axis, which does not account for the size of the receiving country's economy, meaning a small country receiving modest grants could appear to receive little help even if that aid was significant relative to their existing capital. By selecting whichever aid metric produced the desired narrative for each graph, we effectively demonstrated how axis and metric selection alone can tell opposite stories from the same underlying data.

One of the more straightforward parts for us was actually creating the visuals. Since the techniques we used were things we were all familiar with has an easy time implementing them. The difficult part for us was actually performing EDA on our data to find patterns and insights that we could use to create the visuals. Once we were able to uncover those trends the rest was easy. One of the most suprising discoveries we made was how easily we could change the story of our data just by changing our aggregation methods or metric choices without altering any of the underlying data. This showed us that data visualization is more than about just presenting data, but being aware of how it can influence the viewers interpretation and being transparent with those decisions.

In terms of our ethical understanding and analysis throughout the course of this project, we focused on both transparency with the viewer and responsibility in the way we present the findings from our data. We recognized that while our Positive Visualization suggested a trend between foreign aid intensity and health burden indicators, our added annotations and design aim to guide the viewer without insinuating a causation between the two, or any other misleading conclusions to the viewers. The use of meaningful annotations that highlight trends in the data allow for our viewers to easily identify patterns without forcing our conclusion onto them. The simplicity and clarity of our design choices also play a role in ensuring our visualization is clear and effective, rather than complex and confusing.When approaching our "ethical analysis and visualization", we made sure to focus on accuracy of our data, transparency with the viewer, and responsibility to display the data in a way that is neither misleading nor manipulative. Our choices in binning, aggregation, and metric selection were all made with the intent of balancing persuasiveness with transparency, for example our hexile binning points out the overarching trend between foreign aid and health burden indictors while our scatter plot shows the variability in our data in parallel with the overall correlation strength. The methods we used helped us maintain clarity and address the complexity in our data to help viewers identify important patterns without being mislead or confused.
